GLOBALIZATION, LOCALIZATION, AND JAPANESE STUDIES IN THE ASIA-PACIFIC REGION

Event details
International Symposium organized by
International Research Center for Japanese Studies
Department of Japanese Studies, NUS
28 - 30 October 2004
Orchard Parade Hotel, Singapore

Together with the International Research Center for Japanese Studies (Nichibunken), the Department of Japanese Studies of the National University of Singapore (NUS) co-organized an international symposium entitled “Globalization, Localization, and Japanese Studies in the Asia-Pacific Region” on October 28-30 2004 at the Orchard Parade Hotel.

Based on the findings of the symposium organized by Nichibunken and University of Sydney last year, it is hoped that this second conference in Singapore give rise to different perspectives, especially from South East Asian scholars, to enrich the exchange of ideas within the framework of this consideration of globalization, localization, and Japanese Studies in the Asia-Pacific region.

For scholars who specialize on Japan, in particular, this region is believed to offer a rich ground for interdisciplinary exploration of Japan's position and its relations with other nations and cultures. The following main questions were under scrutiny in the conference:

Is it most useful to conceive of Japan as an entity integrated into a single “global system,” or is it more fruitful to think of it as a politically, economically, and culturally active “locality,” one among many “localities”?

What views does Japan hold of the societies and cultures of the Asia-Pacific region as they are framed within the discourse of globalization and localization and how is Japan viewed by these same nations within the same framework?

How have the dynamics of the Asia-Pacific region developed over time, and have these dynamics been transformed by the forces identified as globalism?

What is the significance of these issues for academics studying Japan , or to put it in practical professional terms, how do they act to shape Japanese Studies in the countries in which Japan specialists are employed?

Professors Kawakatsu Heita from Nichibunken and Tan Tai Yong from NUS gave keynote speeches, and more than 40 key Japanese studies specialists from Japan, Malaysia, Australia, Hong Kong, the Philippines, Thailand, Indonesia, Israel and Singapore participated in this symposium.
