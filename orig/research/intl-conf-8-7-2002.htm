INTERNATIONAL CONFERENCE ON JAPANESE MONUMENTS AND MEMORY-MAKING IN JAPAN

Event details
8 - 10 July 2002
Shaw Foundation Building (AS7), Faculty Lounge
Faculty of Arts and Social Sciences
National University of Singapore

Sponsored by Toshiba International Foundation and
The Japan Foundation

Recent socio-cultural theorization has highlighted the importance and complexity of memory as a social construct that endows the past with meaning and, more importantly, enables future action. Memory in this sense constitutes the core of humans as historicized and purposive social agents. It is also essential for groups of all kinds to produce and reproduce themselves by remembering the pastin other words, to construct an identity for themselves. However, such acts of remembering, of self-fashioning is often overlooked, elided and even concealed by the very same agents who engage in them. It is therefore incumbent upon students of history, society and culture to explore processes of collective remembering so as to arrive at a critical understanding of the ideological and sociopolitical maneuvers that social agents undertake to constitute themselves.

Like other post-industrial, consumption-driven societies, Japan mobilizes a great variety of memories in its self-fashioning efforts. Especially postwar Japan has the technology, wealth and popular demand to sustain the cycle of production and consumption of a bewildering range of contending and sometimes conflicting memories. Some of these memories are accorded authority, attaining the status of history or culture, while others have to struggle on the margins as trends, idiosyncrasies or alternative views. Nonetheless, the many ways of remembering in contemporary Japan point to complex and dynamic social forces at work beneath the surface. They open a privileged window onto the internal workings of Japanese society. The aim of this conference is (1) to re-examine Japanese society in light of the latest advances made in the theorizing on social memory and (2) to critique and develop existing theories with the benefit of case studies from Japan.
