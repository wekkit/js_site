EVERYONE'S FESTIVAL? DEMOCRITIZATION AND CONTROL IN KYOTO'S GION MATSURI

Event details
Speaker: Dr Christoph Brumann (Lecturer, Department of Ethnology, University of Cologne)
Date: Friday, 1 November 2002
Time: 3.00 4.30 pm
Venue: AS4/03-028 (JS Staff Meeting Room)

Abstract

One of the "Three Big Festivals" (sandai matsuri) of Japan and perhaps the most famous of all is the Gion matsuri of Kyoto. Evolving from Heian Period roots, the festival has taken on its distinctive and surprisingly unaltered shape in the 16th century, with a large parade (yamaboko junkoo) of 32 floats marking its climax on every July 17th. During the preceding days, each of the floats is assembled by one of the participating neighborhoods in the Muromachi district (the traditional centre of the Japanese kimono wholesale trade). The parade and its preparations give occasion to a stunning display of traditional material culture, attracting hundreds of thousands of spectators.

In the post-war era, the yamaboko junkoo has broadened its scope of participation, support, and meaning. Formerly, it used to be a religious rite sponsored by a handful of wealthy land owners within the participating neighbourhoods. Nowadays, however, it has become a major tourist attraction and a priced piece of national heritage, registered as important cultural property. This justifies the use of large amounts of tax money for defraying the costs. In a participating neighborhood observed during ethnographic fieldwork in 1998/99 and 2001, public sponsorship has given rise to internal democratization. Leadership positions are still assigned by seniority, but wealth has lost most of its significance, and a more open and consultative style of decision-making has emerged. At the same time, however, the neighborhood is forced to deal with the demands of external institutions, such as the central festival committee or the expert commission set up to guard the "traditionality" of the festival. During fieldwork, friction arose over the replacement of time-worn decorative accessories by replicas (instead of new, original creations) and over the allocation of public support money, with the neighborhood being at least partly successful in retaining their control over their festival contribution against controlling agencies.

Retention of neighborhood privilege against outsiders coupled with internal democratization can also be glimpsed behind the events leading to a major innovation in 2001: for the first time in three centuries, women were permitted to participate in the parade. While this decision of the central festival committee was widely welcomed as an adaptation to changed societal circumstances by media commentators, it had little to do with a general inclination towards affirmative action. Rather, one neighborhood had been allowing the clandestine participation of women for several years and did not give in to committee pressure when this was exposed. Within that neighbourhood, female participation again was occasioned less by general principles than by an inability to keep the formerly subservient troop of festival musicians (hayashikata) under control.

Developments such as the construction of high-rise condominiums flooding the Muromachi area with new residents, the gradual attrition in the numbers of long-term residents and the ongoing secularization of the entire festival will very likely sustain the demand for broader participation and control. At the same time, it will be a long time before the participating neighborhoods will entirely lose their prerogatives.

About the speaker

Dr Christoph Brumann did his undergraduate and graduate studies in anthropology, Japanology and Sinology at the University of Cologne. In 1998-99 he was a research fellow at the National Museum of Ethnology in Osaka. Since 1999 he is a Lecturer at the Department of Ethnology, University of Cologne. His research interests include townscape and heritage disputes, communitarian groups/utopian communes, gift exchange, the concept of culture and globalisation.
