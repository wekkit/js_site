WORKING WITH HEGEMONIC MASCULINITY: WORK, MARRIAGE, FATHERHOOD AND THE "SALARYMAN" IN JAPAN

Event details
Speaker: Dr Romit Dasgupta
              Asian Studies, University Western Australia
Date: Wednesday, 31 August 2005
Time: 10:30am - 12:00noon
Venue: AS1/03-04

Abstract

Underlying the process by which Japan emerged as a global industrial power in the twentieth century was a particularly powerful ideology of gender and sexuality that equated masculinity with the public/work sphere and femininity with the private/household sphere. Within this ideological framework, the archetypal male citizen – indeed, the “ideal” male citizen – over the post-World War Two decades came to be represented by the “salaryman” ( sarariiman , in Japanese). Even after the slowing down of economic growth from the 1990s, the salaryman, and all that the discourse of masculinity built up around him represented, has continued to exert a powerful presence on the social landscape. This is despite the fact that, even at the high-point of economic growth in the 1960s and 1970s, only a minority of men would have fallen within the strictest definitional parameters of the term. However, it was the discourse associated with the salaryman – one infused with the gender ideology of the married, heterosexual father/husband breadwinner – that was far more extensive in its reach. In this respect the form of masculinity associated with the salaryman may be regarded as what R.W. Connell terms “hegemonic masculinity”.

Yet, the reality is that this hegemonic model is (and has always been) far more tenuous and slippery than often believed to be the case. As Connell argues, while the hegemonic discourse of masculinity may be the culturally exalted one, it “need not be the most common form . . . let alone the most comfortable . . . many men live in a state of tension with, or distance from, the hegemonic masculinity of their culture or community” (Connell 2000: 11). Hegemonic masculinity, like any form of masculinity is a process, that is constantly “crafted” and “re-crafted”, both at the “macro” level of society, and at the level of the individual. This process of “crafting” involves a variety of negotiations and engagements between the individual and the hegemonic “ideals” – negotiations and engagements which, on closer examination, reveal a far greater degree of ambiguity, tension, and contradiction, than initial appearance would suggest. Specifically, the engagements with the expectations of hegemonic masculinity may incorporate dynamics of both conformity and reinforcement of the dominant ideology, and subversion, undermining, or appropriation of it.

My paper, drawing upon research conducted in Japan with young males who had just entered the workforce, explores some of these engagements with specific reference to hegemonic expectations of the male as husband, father, and breadwinner. It looks at the ways in which these young men –single, about-to-be married, married, heterosexual, non-heterosexual – “craft” their own masculine identities vis-à-vis the expectations of the dominant ideology.

References :
Connell, R.W. 2000. The Men and the Boys . St. Leonards, NSW: Allen and Unwin.

About the speaker

Dr Romit Dasgupta lectures in Japanese, Japanese Studies and Asian Studies in the Discipline of Asian Studies in the School of Social and Cultural Studies at the University of Western Australia . He has recently completed a PhD from Curtin University in Western Australia on the construction of masculinities in the corporate sector in Japan ; the thesis, Crafting Masculinity: Negotiating Masculine Identities in the Japanese Workplace was awarded a Chancellor's Commendation. His research interests include forms of popular culture in Asia and in diasporic Asian communities; constructions of genders and sexualities (in particular, masculinities) in Japan and Asia ; and negotiations of "in-between" identities. He has published in Japanese Studies , Japanese Cybercultures (Routledge: 2003); East Asian Masculinities: The Meaning and Practice of Manhood in China and Japan , (RoutledgeCurzon: 2003). The Encyclopedia of Contemporary Japanese Culture (Routledge: 2002). Together with Dr Mark McLelland of the University of Queensland , he is the co-editor of the recently published volume Genders, Transgenders and Sexualities in Japan (Routledge: August 2005).
