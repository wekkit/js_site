Moving with “No Place to Go”: Gendered Job (Im)Mobility in Japan

Event details
Speaker : Dr Chris McMorran
               Department of Japanese Studies, NUS
Date      : Wednesday, 27 April 2011
Time      : 2:00pm - 4:00pm
Venue    : AS7/01-01

Abstract

Are we living in the age of mobility?  Recent scholarship across the social sciences claims this is the case.  Studies abound on the sped-up movement of capital, technologies, ideas, and a host of mobile figures like tourists, businesspeople, migrants, and refugees.  But is mobility only about movement?  What does all this movement mean, and what practices enable or restrict the mobility of ourselves and others?  This presentation aims to answer these questions and to complicate the idea of mobility by focusing on the workers who reproduce a purposefully immobile place in Japan, the traditional inn, or ryokan.  Based on 12 months of ethnographic research and work in a handful of inns in southern Japan, I showcase women who move great distances to acquire jobs reproducing a home away from home for tourists, yet who say they have "no place to go."  By utilizing recent mobility scholarship that emphasizes practice, this presentation provides insights into gendered labor and the shifting contours of tourism and work in contemporary Japan, as well as makes contributions to wider discourses on mobility within geography and beyond.

About the speaker

Dr Chris McMorran is a cultural geographer with interests in tourism, labor, and the politics of place in contemporary Japan.  He has published articles and book chapters on heritage tourism, the geographies of marginalization and exclusion, and research methodologies.  He teaches modules on Japanese political economy, environment-society relations, and tourism, including a new field study module on ecotourism that will be held in Kyushu in May 2011.  He is currently Visiting Fellow in the Department of Japanese Studies at NUS.  His work has been supported by the Japan Foundation, the National Science Foundation (U.S.), the Social Science Research Council (U.S.), and the Japanese government (MEXT).
