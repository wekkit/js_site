GLOBALISATION, INTERNATIONAL LABOUR MIGRATION IN CONTEMPORARY JAPAN, AND THE LANGUAGE BARRIER: THE INTERNATIONAL RELATIONS OF INTERPRETING JUSTICE

Event details
Speaker: Mr Tomonori Taki (PhD candidate, Department of Politics and International Studies, University of Warwick)
Date: Wednesday, 13 November 2002
Time: 3.00 4.30 pm
Venue: AS4/03-28 (JS Staff Meeting Room)

Abstract

The aim of this presentation is to discuss whether it has been possible for a sector of the Japanese state, which is primarily in charge of domestic political and legal functions, to reformulate its operations in the face of globalisation forces driven by international economic activities. The objective of this research project is to analyse this problem through an investigation of the ways in which Japans criminal justice administration addressed the obstacles to dealing with the issue of foreigner related crime in the early 1990s. Although the manifold impact of the arrival of migrant workers in Japan in the last two decades of the post-war period has already been researched to some degree, issues relating to administration of criminal justice to non-Japanese speaking defendants is still understudied. In Japan at the end of the 1980s there was increasing concern that the lack of sufficient communication between foreigners suspected of the commission of crime on the one hand, and the officials of the criminal justice system on the other, was causing serious problems of justice and public order. Civil society actors and the states institutions were both aware of these problems, and the former in particular argued that the foreign suspects and defendants were placed at a great disadvantage under the justice system. In this presentation, the speaker seeks to analyse, in three steps, the language barrier problems which arose from the fact that the states officials and foreigners accused of crime often spoke different languages in the process of administering justice. Firstly, the speaker provides a brief outline of the trends in foreigner crimes as reported in Japans official statistics. Secondly, he provides an overview of the legal and political implications of the language barrier problems that face the Japanese state. Thirdly, he considers the way in which the Japanese state has reacted and devised policy responses to the language barrier problem. Following on these findings, the speaker considers whether the Japanese state has been in retreat in the face of globalisation forces, or whether it has managed to readjust its institutions and practices to manage this new environment. The presentation will also seek to explore the broader implications of Japans response to migration in terms of modes of understanding Japans international relations, the impact of globalisation and migration on states, and international relations in East Asia and in other regions more generally. Finally, the presentation will also consider the implications of the case of migration in Japan for changing understandings of individual human security.

About the speaker

Mr Tomonori Taki is a PhD candidate at the Department of Politics and International Studies, University of Warwick, UK. He graduated from the Faculty of Economics, Yokohama National University, Japan, and has made presentations in UK, USA and Japan.
