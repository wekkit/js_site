REPUTATION AND REASSURANCE IN INTERNATIONAL POLITICS:
THE CASE OF JAPAN IN EAST ASIA

Event details

Speaker : Assoc Prof Paul Midford
               Department of Political Science and Sociology,
               Norwegian University for Science and Techology
Date      : Friday, 17 August 2007
Time      : 2:15pm - 4:15pm
Venue    : AS4/03-28 (JS Meeting Room)

Abstract

Can states acquire reputations? Can they acquire reputations for being aggressive or benign? Is a reputation for aggressiveness an asset or a liability? In this talk I argue that the state can acquire a reputation for aggressiveness and that this is a liability. The state with such a reputation has an incentive to reassure others about its disposition, thereby discouraging balancing efforts aimed at the state. Japan in post-war East Asia well illustrates the logic of reputation and reassurance in international politics. Because of Japan's conquest and often brutal rule of much of East Asia it found itself saddled with a reputation for aggressiveness. Changes in its security policy that would appear benign in the case of other states were often interpreted as indicators of aggressiveness in the light of pre-1945 behavior. However, Japan 's reputation has varied according to the wartime experience of the perceiver, with Taiwan and Thailand, for example, having a relatively benign view based upon a comparably benign occupation experience. I find that Japan has engaged in both military and historical reassurance in order to improve its reputation as a military power in East Asia. During the Cold War Japan's reassurance strategy rested on three pillars: self-containment with the framework of the war-renouncing Article 9 and the US alliance, a defensive defense military doctrine, and a reassurance diplomacy embodied in the 1977 Fukuda doctrine. Since the end of the Cold War Japan has improved its reputation as a military power by dispatching its military overseas to participate in UN peacekeeping and provide humanitarian and reconstruction relief overseas. Overall, Japan's military reassurance strategy has been reasonably successful. However, its attempts at historical reassurance have been less successful due to its tardiness and recent changes in Japanese domestic politics. Finally, I briefly spell out the implications for other comparable cases, including Germany in Europe and the United States globally.

About the speaker

Paul Midford is an Associate Professor in the Department of Political Science and Sociology, and Director of the Japan Program at the Norwegian University for Science and Technology (NTNU) in Trondheim. He is also Associate Editor at Japan Forum. He has published in International Organization, Security Studies, Asian Survey, The Pacific Review, and the International Relations of the Asia Pacific. Most recently Midford published Japanese Public Opinion and the War on Terrorism: Implications for Japan's Security Strategy, Policy Study #27 (East-West Center Washington office, 2006). Previously, he taught at Kwansei Gakuin University, Lafayette College , and Kanazawa University. Midford has a Ph.D. in political science from Columbia University.
