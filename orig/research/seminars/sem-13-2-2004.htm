MEMORY, MIRRORS AND MISSING WOMEN: METAFICTIVE NARRATIVE STRATEGIES AND THE DOPPELGANGER MOTIF IN THE FICTIONS OF ABE KB AND MURAKAMI HARUKI

Event details
Speaker: Mr Baryon Tensor Posadas (MA candidate, Department of Japanese Studies, NUS)
Date: 13 February 2004, Friday
Time: 2:00pm 3:30pm
Venue: AS4/03-28 (JS Staff Meeting Room)

Abstract

Since the early twentieth century, the dominant paradigm in the categorization of Japanese fictions has been the dichotomy between pure literature (junbungaku) and mass literature (taishbungaku). The problems of this reductive dichotomy are highlighted by the narrative strategies deployed in the fictions of two writers: Abe K b (1924-1993) and Murakami Haruki (1948-) wherein the mimesis of pure literature and the formulaic structures of ����mass literature are simultaneously parodied and subverted.

As a result of their generational difference, however, it is rare to find these two writers discussed side by side. Nevertheless, a close examination of their works reveals a number of similarities in their narrative strategies. In particular, their subversion of the confessional mode and deployment of fantastic and hard-boiled fictional structures often lead to their texts exhibiting unreliable and metafictional characteristics. Furthermore, two other motifs that recur in the novels of both Abe and Murakami are linked to this narrative strategy: the emergence of a doppelganger figure and the image of the absent female.

In this presentation, I will examine these three recurring narrative strategies as they are articulated in four novels, namely Abes Face of Another (Tanin no kao, 1963) and The Box-man (Hakootoko, 1973) along with Murakamis Hard-boiled Wonderland and the End of the World (Sekai no owari to hdoboirudo wand rando, 1985) and Sputnik Sweetheart (Suptoniku no koibito, 1999). Alienated and narcissistic protagonists who, in one way or another, are obsessed with a female figure that has been rendered either symbolically or literally absent narrate all four novels. Echoing the fragmented and self-referential nature of the narrative, their situations are further complicated by the fragmentation of their selves highlighted by the emergence of their doubles, doppelganger figures that tend to embody a more instinctual, aggressive persona.

Through my readings of these four texts, I intend to demonstrate the simultaneous reflecting and responding to the discourse on the disintegration of representation and subjectivity resulting from the simulation and routinization of language. I situate my readings of the various manifestations of these narrative strategies and motifs presented within them into the discourses and debates on Japans contemporary urban modernity and hope to suggest possible perspectives into these discourses.

About the speaker

Baryon Tensor Posadas is a MA candidate at the Department of Japanese Studies, NUS. He completed his undergraduate degree in English Studies (Creative Writing) at the University of the Philippines, Diliman. Alongside pursuing his research interests in postwar Japanese fiction and cultural studies, Mr. Posadas is also an active fictionist.
