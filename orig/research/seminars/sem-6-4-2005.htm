CONTEMPORARY FUNERAL INDUSTRY IN JAPAN AND FAMILY CHANGES

Event details
Speaker: Dr Hikari Suzuki (Singapore Management University)
Date: 6 April 2005, Thursday
Time: 3:00pm-4:30pm
Venue: ARI Seminar Room (AS7/04-09)

Abstract

This paper examines the shifting fate of Japanese elders. With extreme demographic changes taking place in Japan , elders are facing concerns of death and dying individually than earlier generations. Economic development also shifted the filial piety values on both the aging and younger people. More and more elders feel they cannot depend on their children while many children, namely daughter-in-laws, feel that taking care of in-laws is not fair. The changes of values have brought marketing of pre-need funeral arrangement services by funeral companies, bringing funeral companies into a scene where it was very much a family matter. Elderly are also facing lack of network and close relationships after retirement. Longevity as a consequence, has come to place Japanese elders in isolation and disengagement for longer period of time.

As Japan further enters into aging society, a president of a funeral company, Moon Ray, realized its risks of isolated elders and decided to take action. His ideal of Grand City is a leading attempt to create a segmented-community constructed by both the funeral company and local elders. Along the line with Traphagan and Knight, I deny the convergence theory or Westernization according to which Japanese society is seen to be socially, economically, and culturally converging with western culture. I argue that “Japan is modern but retains distinctive characteristics” (Traphagan & Knight 2003:6) with regarding to dealing with their elders and aging society.

About the speaker

Suzuki, Hikaru is currently an assistant professor of anthropology at the Singapore Management University. She is the author of The Price of Death: The Funeral Industry in Contemporary Japan (Stanford, 2000). This work investigated the transformation and professionalization of funeral practices in Japan. Suzuki received her BA in Chinese History from Beijing University. She then received her Ph.D. in Anthropology from Harvard University. She continued her education at the University of Wisconsin-Madison where she received an MBA in Marketing. Her future research interests include environmental management, environmental globalization, transformation of capitalism, business impact on the changes of cultural practices, the relationship between changing communities and professionalization.
