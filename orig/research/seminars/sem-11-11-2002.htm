REFRAMING JAPANESE CITIZENS' MOVEMENTS: FROM ACCUSATION TO PROPOSAL

Event details
Speaker: Mr Simon Avenell (PhD candidate, Department of History, University of California, Berkeley)
Date: Monday, 11 November 2002
Time: 3.00 4.30 pm
Venue: AS4/03-28 (JS Staff Meeting Room)

Abstract

From the mid-1970s Japanese activists began to transform the prevailing logic of citizens movements. Rejecting what they called the accusation-style of 1960s social movements, core activists conceptualized a new proposal-style of citizen activism. Instead of accusing the state or corporate Japan of authoritarianism or environmental destruction, they suggested that citizens should channel their energies into the creation of alternative systems which in time would lay the foundation for a differentand perhaps betterJapan.

This presentation explores the dynamics of this transformation from accusation to proposal via the example of the Association to Preserve the Earth an organic vegetable distribution company formed in 1974. The speaker argues that the ideas of core activists in this movement are crucial in explaining its organizational form and business practices. By drawing on cultural resources from earlier movements, by framing their ideas to resonate with constituent group identities, and by skillfully identifying new opportunities for activism, core activists successfully mobilized and developed a new generation of civil society organizations. While accusation-style movements continued to exist, they were now supplemented by a new realm of alternative activism.

The speaker addresses four issues in the presentation. First, what were the dynamics of proposal- style movements? Second, how do we situate this new style of proposal activism within the wider history of postwar citizens movements in Japan? Third, how does my approach build on explanations of citizens movements to date? And fourth, what were the long-term impacts of proposal-style activism for Japanese civil society?

About the speaker

Simon Avenell is a doctoral candidate in the Department of History at the University of California, Berkeley. He completed undergraduate studies at the University of Queensland, Australia, and International Christian University, Japan. His research deals with the development of Japanese citizens movements and civil society from 1960 to the present, with particular emphasis on the role of ideas in the mobilization and development of movements. He has been a visiting researcher at the Institute for Social Science at the University of Tokyo since October 2000, and is currently researching the emergence of Japanese NPOs from the late 1990s.
