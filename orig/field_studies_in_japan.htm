Field Studies in Japan (access the complete blog here)


In May 2011 the Department of Japanese Studies conducted its inaugural field studies module in Japan.  Dr Chris McMorran led the field study, the theme of which was Ecotourism.  The field study began with one week of instruction at NUS on ecotourism and field methods, as well as case studies of the locations to be visited, all of which helped students understand Japan's environmental past and future.  Then, we traveled to the island of Kyushu from 17-26 May so that students could experience the complex relationships between tourism and the environment for themselves.

The field study began at Huis ten Bosch, the Dutch theme park in Nagasaki that boasts an extensive recycling network and green power generation system under its streets.

 

Next, we visited Isahaya Bay, location of recent public protest over the environmental impacts of a large land reclamation project.

 

Then, we visited the city of Minamata, location of mercury poisoning in the 1950s and 60s, which has rebuilt its contaminated bay into a recreation area and declared itself a “Model City for the Environment”.

 

Finally, we explored several villages around the volcanic region of Mt Aso, where entrepreneurs have developed farmstays that grow organic food, inns that feature natural hot springs, and even a tourism university that organizes workshops for students, government officials and business owners curious about eco-tourism.

 

Along the way we heard from entrepreneurs, non-profit groups, and public officials trying to balance environmental goals with economic and political realities.  Participants also had an exchange with Japanese University students, experienced a homestay and a farmstay, visited an historic castle, and went white-water rafting.

Upon completion of the field study, the students presented their impressions in a public forum.  The event, held at the Japan Creative Centre, was called "Green Tourism in Japan: creative approaches."  In front of an audience of 70 people, student participants gave their impressions of ecotourism efforts in Japan and shared ideas about what ideas might be borrowed to make Singapore more environmentally-friendly.  The audience included representatives from the Embassy of Japan, JNTO (Japan National Tourism Organization), JTB (Japan Travel Bureau), JCCI (Japanese Chamber of Commerce and Industry, Singapore), and CLAIR (Council of Local Authorities for International Relations), as well as a number of educational institutions like NUS and NTU.  This was an excellent example of bridging knowledge from the classroom to the community.

 

The field study was generously funded by the National University of Singapore's Office of the Vice-President (University and Global Relations) (VPUGR) and Faculty of Arts and Social Sciences, the Japanese Chamber of Commerce and Industry (Singapore), and the JENESYS Programme of the Japanese Government.

Please direct any questions to Dr Chris McMorran (jpscmm@nus.edu.sg).
